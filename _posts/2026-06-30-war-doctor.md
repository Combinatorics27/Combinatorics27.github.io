War Doctor is a book by Dr. David Nott from UK about his experiences of being a volunteer surgeon at places of war. 

these are my thoughts in no particular order 

- the author mentions that he is 'middle-class' in the UK. he then goes on to mention that at the last year of his medical course, he was allowed to go on an 'elective' - a chance to study overseas. He chose to go to Singapore, Malaysia, Thailand and Burma.
  - its funny and scary. basis my knowledge of indian macro-statistics through MoSPI and other surveys, i probably am the 'rich class' in India. yet going to 4 countries for study is still a far-fetched reality. possible, but unlikely. its striking. this inequality. i am the beneficiary of inequality if we consider India. but if we zoom out and look at the world, I then become the unlucky lot. probably a helpful thing is that the brain takes relative status as input so when I'm in India, i'm happy. that's not very true when I introspect a handful of times i'm a tourist at foreign countries.
  - also a point worth considering is that infrastructure and resources play a huge role in individual human development. makes me ever more pessimistic about our country improving massively to match the west.
 
- author mentions that surgery in the 1980s was a trial of sleep deeprivation and that they worked an average of 140 hours per week.
  - this is true of India as well. residencies are brutal. i've heard stories from friends and relatives about not being able to go to their rooms for 3 days straight. i have no idea whether this helps (by training the doctors) or is harmful (by detoriating mental and physical health of future doctors)
 
- sonder. the author mentions how once when going to a war zone, the plane they were in had to nose dive to land. what this made me realise was how so many different people work togethor, even in such difficult environments. i know the doctor's story but what's also worth considering is the pilot. who did these nose-dives regularly.

- ***The endorphin rush of hearing and feeling bullets and missiles whizz overhead was like nothing I'd encountered before, and everyday life seemed humdrum by comparison***
  - this is truly interesting. humans lives have been violent ever since the beginning. despite a couple of wars ongoing currently, we live in one of the most peaceful times in human history. there's a lot of 'experiencing' that we don't know about. how the body feels and reacts in violent situations is something that almost every human knew before but most now don't.
  - this is probably why the author couldn't stop volunteering. his dayjob in london must have felt boring after experiencing so many emotions chronically in warzones.
 
- ***Usually it's when you come back from a war zone and the end of a mission that you are struck by the banality of life, and how ridiculous petty and pointless disputes like this seem.***
  - the author says this after mentioning a story about how his copassengers in a plane fought about reclining the seat.
  - its funny. this difference of problems. most of our problems are, in the absolute scale, trivial. but the issue with the brain is that it doesn't care about the absolute scale. it think in relative terms. so if you're a priviledged westerner, even seat reclining becomes as big a problem to make it worth fighting. humans in warzones would laugh if they saw a westener go through their life worry about trivial problems.
  - if you do not give your brain hard problems to solve, it'll make trivial things hard.
 
- ***Wading in and barking orders helps no one: you have to build relationships, and trust, before anyone will beocome open to accepting that there might be a better way of doing things.***

- ***A fairly routine diagnostic process for me was revelatory for the Syrian surgeons.***
  - biology is complex. truly complex. lawyers and accountants are called professionals just like doctors. but doctors operate in nature's territory. lawyers and accountants read stuff that is made by humans. nature is not constrained by human cognition. therefore biology is magnitudes more complex than law or accounts can ever be.
 
- author mentions a system where each patient had a video cam and vitals streamed to a doctor in USA who would then prescribe further treatments that would be executed by the local nurse. this seems like a nice solution to the lack of doctors in warzones. this techinque should be replicable in other white collar fields where knowledge is the bottleneck.

- ***....arteriovenous fistula - a connection between the artery and the vein caused by the bullet.***

- the author mentions an incident where a patient had come with a condition that he knew how to operate about but wanted to teach the local doctors the procedure. he supervised while the local doctor did the surgery. the patient died.
  - it makes me really think about knowledge transfer in fields like surgery. you need to train junior doctors, yes. but how do you go about it knowing that humans will make mistakes. all professionals do. but the difference between accountants and doctors is that one's mistake will cause you a couple thousand bucks in tax and the other's might cause you your life.
  - so how does one ensure knowledge transfer without sacrificing service quality? if i want to get a surgery done, i'd want it to be totally done by the senior most doctor and that the resident or junior does not try any training on me.
  - also related is how doctor's mistakes are foregone in india. the author mentions how in UK even tiny mistakes cause negligence lawsuits. that's not correct either. but there needs to be accountability. in india, i don't see it. but then i've read stories about how patients shout at doctors and nurses. yeah so maybe the accountability in india is through mental torture instead of courts.

- the above incident that i mentioned. the patient was a young boy. the author went outside to update his father. his father said that he had lost his other two sons in the same attack and was sure god would protect his only surviving child.
  - life is drastically different at different geographies.
  - what people go through in warzones is really unimaginable (even unimaginable is an understatement) for the people in developed nations.
 
- the author mentions that hummus and bread was a staple at one place.
  - it made me realise how hummus in india is actually a luxury food. hummus is a staple in a warzone yet is a luxury food in india. i don't know what to make of it but its probably worth thinking about.
 
- the author mentions how when he was in london, a patient came with the issue of visible arteries. it is only an aesthetic issue and requires an injection to be administered. when the doctor mentioned he was going to a warzone next week, the patient said that he must be taking a lot of these injections with him. obviously the patient was unaware.
  - this is interesting because this means that most people don't even know the extent of problems their fellow species members go through.
