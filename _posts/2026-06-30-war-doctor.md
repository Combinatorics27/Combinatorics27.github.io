War Doctor is a book by Dr. David Nott from UK about his experiences of being a volunteer surgeon at places of war. 

these are my thoughts in no particular order 

- the author mentions that he is 'middle-class' in the UK. he then goes on to mention that at the last year of his medical course, he was allowed to go on an 'elective' - a chance to study overseas. He chose to go to Singapore, Malaysia, Thailand and Burma.
  - its funny and scary. basis my knowledge of indian macro-statistics through MoSPI and other surveys, i probably am the 'rich class' in India. yet going to 4 countries for study is still a far-fetched reality. possible, but unlikely. its striking. this inequality. i am the beneficiary of inequality if we consider India. but if we zoom out and look at the world, I then become the unlucky lot. probably a helpful thing is that the brain takes relative status as input so when I'm in India, i'm happy. that's not very true when I introspect a handful of times i'm a tourist at foreign countries.
  - also a point worth considering is that infrastructure and resources play a huge role in individual human development. makes me ever more pessimistic about our country improving massively to match the west.
 
- author mentions that surgery in the 1980s was a trial of sleep deeprivation and that they worked an average of 140 hours per week.
  - this is true of India as well. residencies are brutal. i've heard stories from friends and relatives about not being able to go to their rooms for 3 days straight. i have no idea whether this helps (by training the doctors) or is harmful (by detoriating mental and physical health of future doctors)
 
- sonder. the author mentions how once when going to a war zone, the plane they were in had to nose dive to land. what this made me realise was how so many different people work togethor, even in such difficult environments. i know the doctor's story but what's also worth considering is the pilot. who did these nose-dives regularly.
