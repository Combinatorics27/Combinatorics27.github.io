suppose my dog had stomach pain. one rational action for him would be to come to me and say 'i have stomach pain. take me to the vet.'. but obviously we realise that that action is outside the set of possible actions for the dog. he lacks the cognition. the most rational possible decision for him is then to cry and be sad to communicate the message hoping that i can infer it. same is with human children.

it'd be irrational if a human adult was just crying and being sad instead of saying what's wrong. humans are the leaders in cognition in the set of creatures that we know. but obviously that set is not exhaustive (could be, but really nominal probability) and therefore much of what we do would seem irrational to a higher being. 

rationality, i'll define to be chosing the action from your action set that maximises your utility. this definition brings 2 ways a person X could be making a rational decision but which can be interpreted as irratinal through the eyes of some other person Y - 

1. Y has more actions in their action set than X. an adult human has complicated language, a dog doesn't. 
2. Y has a different utility assigned to actions. simple example that most of us can understand easily is that while you might love cookies and cream, you do not expect everyone else to buy cookies and cream ice cream. this gets difficult to understand with other life decisions such as why wouldn't you want to marry, or have kids or run after money? the answer is that different people can have different utility functions. our brains are prey to false consensus effect.

what is rational for you is not necessarily rational for others. and vice versa.
