the heckman equation tells that the return on investment for education is significantly more in the early years. investing in early education is more beneficial than investing in later stage education.

the mathematical way to understand this is knowledge at time t+1 = f(knowledge at t, investment at t). knowledge, like skills builds on earlier acquired knowledge. the early few years of a human are vital for later development. neuroscience tells us that the brains of children are highly plastic and a good education at earlier ages is really important for development at later ages.

knowledge also compounds. the neuroscientific explanation for this is schematic learning. the brain makes schemas or pattern-groups. if something new is similar to a previously learnt schema, the brain quickly identifies that and learns the new knowledge quickly. so for example, it will take much more time to learn how to ride a bicycle than it'll take to then learn how to ride a motorbike. 

the great gatsby curve is a graph that illustrates the relationship between wealth inequality and intergenerational mobility. intergenerational mobility measures how much a person's success is dependent on their parental wealth. the crucial finding of the great gatsby curve is that in unequal countries, the intergenerational mobility is lower, which means, that children will end up in the same economic class as their parents. rich kids will go on to become rich adults whereas the poor will stay poor. 

specifically in the indian context, this could mean that a successful person is more probably successful because their parents were successful rather than due to their individual efforts. 

the heckman equation partly explains the cause of the curve. in unequal countries, poor kids do not have access to decent education. for that reason, they grow on to not become valuable human capital and therefore remain poor. whereas the rich kids in unequal countries send their children to the best institutes of their countries or abroad.
